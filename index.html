Our Solar System

NASA notes that our solar system has eight planets, and the first four are rocky terrestrial worlds while Jupiter and Saturn are gas giants and Uranus and Neptune are ice giants. The solar system also includes dwarf planets, hundreds of moons, asteroids, and comets. [web:1][web:2]

Solar System Overview

Our solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a cloud of gas and dust. Venus is the hottest planet, Jupiter is the largest, Mercury and Venus have no moons, and the outer planets have no hard surfaces like the terrestrial planets do. [web:1][web:2]

PlanetsMercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune
MoonsHundreds of moons orbit planets and dwarf planets
FormationAbout 4.6 billion years ago
Special factVenus is hotter than Mercury

Galaxy Explorer

NASA describes galaxies as huge collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter bound by gravity. They come in major shapes like spiral, elliptical, lenticular, and irregular, and the Milky Way is a spiral galaxy more than 100,000 light-years across. [web:21][web:22][web:23]

The Milky Way

Our home galaxy is a spiral galaxy with a disk of stars spanning more than 100,000 light-years, and Earth sits along one of its spiral arms about halfway from the center. The Milky Way is part of the Local Group and contains a supermassive black hole at its center like most large galaxies. [web:21][web:23][web:26]

TypeBarred spiral galaxy
SizeMore than 100,000 light-years across
LocationEarth lies in one spiral arm
GroupMember of the Local Group
